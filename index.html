Wake County's Housing Crisis

2018

Increasing Demand

An average of 67 new residents move to Wake County each day, making it one of the fastest growing counties in North Carolina. An additional 170,000 people have flooded the county in the past eight years, straining affordability and intensifying demand for all types of housing.

There is already a deficit of 50,000 units of affordable housing across Wake County. The crisis is projected to only worsen through 2030 — to provide for the anticipated influx, an additional 7,500 new housing units must be built each year.

—Wake County Commissioner Jessica Holmes,
Wake County Commissioner Sig Hutchinson,
The News & Observer

Affordability

For those who manage to secure housing, affordability becomes the next hurdle.

Affordability is a nationwide problem. In no state can a two-bedroom apartment at the Fair Market Rent be afforded by the federal minimum wage.

But the affordability crisis is especially critical in Wake County.

Apartment prices in Wake County are over 20 percent higher than the state average. Forty-three percent of renters and 19 percent of homeowners report having difficulty affording housing.

The Effects

With shrinking supply and surging prices, many Wake County residents resort to dilapidated, unsafe housing conditions. Particularly vulnerable populations include seniors, veterans, the mentally ill, single women, children and low-income families.

Left with no viable alternatives, residents jeopardize health when residing in spaces without regular heating and air conditioning, water supply or pest control.

“You have people living in all kinds of harsh situations because you don’t have affordable housing,” said Octavia Rainey, a housing advocate. “And that leads to all kinds of health issues, stress, hypertensions and people not taking care of themselves. If you’re not healthy, you can’t work.”

Homelessness is another growing concern in Wake County. There were 4,726 homeless persons in Wake County reported in 2016, including 749 children.
